# Essay 2 — A Time You Learned From a Mistake or Setback

**Status:** Draft from Luca. See note at bottom before final polish.

---

One production at MSA taught me something about leading kids that I couldn't have learned any other way.

A group of kids started filming their stop-motion video without writing a script first — they just wanted to start building and shooting. I'd told them beforehand that they needed a script, but they didn't listen. They went ahead anyway, filmed a bunch of footage, and when we watched it back, it was messy. The stop-motion was all over the place, the story didn't make sense, and we couldn't post it.

So we stopped, reset, and told them they had to write a script before they could keep going.

What I learned wasn't that scripts are important — I already knew that. What I learned was how kids actually believe things. They didn't trust that a script mattered until they saw their own video fall apart without one. Being told wasn't enough. They had to experience it.

After that, I changed how I run productions. Now, before kids start any project, we watch examples from our own MSA YouTube channel — real videos we've made, ones that work. Kids see what good looks like. And I'm firmer about the script rule. It's not a suggestion anymore; it's a requirement before anyone touches a camera.

That lesson stuck. And it's why, this summer, I built a Claude-powered script builder for MSA — a tool that helps kids turn a vague idea into a real plan before they ever touch a camera. Because kids learn by seeing what happens when you skip the planning step. This tool makes that planning step easier, so fewer kids have to learn the hard way.

The lesson: show, don't tell. Let them learn by doing. Then they're convinced, not just compliant.

**Polish note (before submission):** The prompt asks about a time *you* made a
mistake. Right now the mistake reads as the kids not listening. One small shift
makes it land harder: name your own misjudgment explicitly — e.g., "My mistake
was assuming that telling them once was leading. It wasn't." The rest of the
essay already supports that framing.
